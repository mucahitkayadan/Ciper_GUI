Caesar Cipher Overview

Introduction

The Caesar cipher is a basic substitution cipher where each letter in the plaintext is shifted a fixed number of positions down the alphabet.

How It Works

The shift value, also known as the key, determines the substitution. For example, with a shift of 3, 'A' becomes 'D', 'B' becomes 'E', and so on.

Encryption Process

To encrypt a message using the Caesar cipher, each letter in the plaintext is shifted by the chosen key. Spaces and non-alphabetic characters remain unchanged.

Decryption Process

To decrypt, the receiver uses the same key to shift each letter in the opposite direction, effectively reversing the encryption process.

Security Considerations

The Caesar cipher is vulnerable to brute force attacks, especially due to its limited number of possible keys (the 26 letters of the alphabet).

It is a relatively weak form of encryption and is not suitable for securing sensitive information.

Conclusion

The Caesar cipher is a historical encryption method with a simple mechanism. While it played a role in ancient cryptography, modern applications require more secure encryption techniques.
